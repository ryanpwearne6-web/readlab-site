Seven everyday moments

A question for every part of the day

You don't need a big talk. You need the right small question at the right moment. Pick the moment, ask just one — one question per conversation is enough.

🍽️ At dinner
"What was the hardest part of your day?"

Teaches them to name difficulty without fear — so they stop carrying it alone.

🌙 At bedtime
"What made you laugh today?"

Ends the day scanning for joy instead of replaying what went wrong.

🎒 After school
"Was anyone left out today?"

Children who notice exclusion become children who include.

☀️ In the morning
"Who might need a friend today?"

Gives them a purpose beyond themselves — they arrive braver.

💙 When they're upset
"Where do you feel that in your body?"

Locating a feeling makes it manageable instead of overwhelming.

Why it works

Small questions, real development

These aren't just nice chats. Each question quietly builds the social and emotional skills children carry for life — grounded in how their developing brains work.

🫧

Naming a feeling calms it

When a child names or locates an emotion, the thinking part of the brain comes online and big feelings become manageable. "Name it to tame it" is a skill — and it's trainable.

🤝

Empathy is trained, not born

Questions like "how do you think they felt?" build the habit of seeing through someone else's eyes. Repeated often, perspective-taking becomes second nature.

🌱

Resilience grows from ownership

Asking "which part can't you do?" instead of solving it hands the problem back. Children build an internal compass and the belief that they can find their own way through.

💬

Connection is the foundation

Ten seconds of the right question, every day, builds the secure, trusting relationship that everything else — confidence, learning, reading — grows from.

About Read Lab

Hi, I'm the teacher behind Read Lab.

I'm a passionate primary teacher who believes the best learning happens through authentic, strong relationships — and that the way we talk to children shapes who they become.

In the classroom I noticed the questions teachers ask do something ordinary conversation doesn't: they build emotional awareness, empathy and resilience. Parents kept asking how I do it, so I turned it into simple cards any family can use at the dinner table.

"The goal isn't to get your child to talk more. It's to change what they talk about."

As a (relatively new!) proud parent myself, I know how little time there is. Read Lab is built for real family life — one small question at a time. And when your child is ready to read, those resources are here too.
